Smart, Quiet, & Hardworking
Stereotype of Asian Americans Perpetuates Anti-Blackness


By Irene Vailikit

Asians, On Average

The “smart and quiet” stereotype of the Asian American student has rung familiar in classrooms and pop culture throughout the United States for decades. Coined the “model minority,” Asian Americans collectively score higher on standardized tests and attain bachelor’s degrees at higher rates than other racial groups. Additionally, they seemingly achieve the “American Dream” with the highest median annual income by race. Below are the statistics of Asian American achievement shown as an aggregate.

Asians Are Not All The Same: Disaggregating Data

On the surface, it may seem compelling to believe that if, “Asians can achieve, other minorities can too,” However, the “Asian” race includes over 28 ethnic groups who have immigrated from East Asia, South Asia, Southeast Asia, and Pacific Islands. When disaggregating the data by ethnic group, it becomes apparent that Laotian, Bhutanese, and Hmong groups have lower income and education levels than the national average due to immigration policies and patterns. The myth that all Asian groups are smarter and wealthier than the average American makes it challenging for low-income ethnic groups to gain access to financial and social services.

The Effect on Black Americans and Civil Rights

The model minority myth perpetuates racism and anti-blackness in insidious ways. Historically, the combined effects of immigration policies, biased storytelling, and sensationalized media coverage perpetuate the racist stereotype. The stereotype has been exploited by the government and media to intentionally discredit the black civil rights movement.

The Perpetual Foreigner

Asian Americans have experienced a long history of racism and exclusion.The Chinese Exclusion Act of 1882 and the internment of Japanese Americans during World War II incite the notion of the Asian American as perpetually "foreign" despite the number of generations their family has lived in the U.S.



1952-1965

Professionals Immigrate During Cold War

After all Asian immigrants were banned from the U.S. for 28 years, the Immigration Acts of 1952 and 1965 were passed, preferencing highly educated immigrants. The influx of foreign professionals was expected to support the economic supremacy of the U.S. over communist nations. It provided the foundation for the model minority myth as large numbers of educated Asians immigrated.



1966

"Model Minority" Narrative is Adopted to Shift Responsibility

Minority groups have often attempted to circulate stories portraying themselves as upstanding citizens in order to combat racism. In the mid 1900s, Chinese and Japanese American success stories were adopted more widely by the media than those of African Americans. The model minority narrative was a way for the U.S. to win allies during the Cold War and a convenient response to the civil rights movement; politicians could avoid responsibility for Black poverty and blame Black Americans by comparing them with the seemingly “more successful” Asian groups.



1970

Influx of Immigrants Perpetuate Stereotype

The Asian American population almost tripled after the 1965 Immigration Act. Sociologist Richard Baldoz shares that the immigrants were “Filipino nurses recruited to fill labor shortages..., Chinese and Taiwanese students and scientists fleeing communism, Korean entrepreneurs, and Indian engineers.” This strategic policy, rather than Asian ethnic traits, is the best explanation of Asian American socio-economic success.

80s and 90s Media Continue Narrative

From this 1987 Time Magazine cover to Amy Chua’s 2011 controversial memoir, Battle Hymn of the Tiger Mother, popular media continues to portray Asian Americans as the model minority. However, as discourse about racism has become more mainstream, Asian Americans have started to speak out about its harm.



The Racial Triangulation of Asian Americans

In her 1999 article, “The Racial Triangulation of Asian Americans,” Claire Jean Kim offers a “Field of Racial Positions” as a way to understand how stereotypes of Asian Americans uphold white hegemony. The Field allows readers to compare White, Black, and Asian American racial groups. The Y axis represents relative superiority, while the X axis represents to what extent the group is considered an “insider.”

Hover over the families to learn more.

Asian Americans, perpetually categorized as “foreign,” are othered by Whites and by the media. This othering upholds a narrative that there are foreign ethnic characteristics that make the race superior to Blacks when it comes to education and economic success.

Whites, who hold positions of power and determine our political systems sit at the top of the superiority spectrum, and are considered “insider” or fully American.

Blacks, who have suffered from generations of systemic oppression are situated below Asian Americans on the superiority spectrum.

It is the result of racist policies that has put Blacks at an educational and economic disadvantage. At the same time, broader society endorses the model minority narrative and extols the assumed virtues of Asian Americans. Because there is a minority group that is perceived to be “better than” Blacks, upholding the model minority myth perpetuates anti-Black racism.
